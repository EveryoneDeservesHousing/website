## EDH Proposal

Develop and launch a nonprofit that responds directly to the realities of street-level homelessness. The nonprofit will integrate enhanced shelter, street outreach, physical and behavioral healthcare, recovery and treatment pathways, trauma-informed care, trauma-informed design, and applied research into a single, coordinated model.

The goal is not only to deliver services but also to reimagine how environments, systems, and care models can reduce harm, stabilize people faster, and create clearer pathways to housing, recovery, and long-term health.

## Rationale

Learnings from current models of shelter services demonstrated that integration matters, aligning shelter, housing, healthcare partnerships, behavioral health, and care review leads to better outcomes for people with complex needs. It also surfaced persistent gaps:

- Street-level homelessness requires on-site, immediate access to care rather than referrals alone.
- Behavioral health and substance use treatment are most effective when co-located and continuous, not episodic.
- Shelter environments themselves can unintentionally re-trigger trauma, undermining engagement and recovery.
- Staff experience secondary trauma and need environments designed for safety, regulation, and sustainability.

The nonprofit responds to these lessons by intentionally designing both the service model and the physical environment to support stabilization, dignity, and healing.

## Core Program Elements

### 1. Street-Level Engagement and Pathway to Shelter

The nonprofit will function as a front door for people living unsheltered, offering 24/7 access, basic needs, and immediate stabilization without preconditions. Outreach, shelter, and engagement teams will operate as one coordinated system.

### 2. Integrated Health and Behavioral Healthcare

On-site medical, nursing, and behavioral health services will address urgent needs while supporting continuity of care. This includes mental health treatment, substance use services, medication support, and strong partnerships for higher-acuity care.

### 3. Continuum of Recovery and Treatment

Rather than a binary "in treatment / not in treatment" approach, the nonprofit will support a continuum, which could include medication-assisted treatment, contingency management, recovery groups, and pathways into more intensive treatment when people are ready.

### 4. Trauma-Informed Care and Workforce Support

Care delivery will be grounded in trauma-informed practice, recognizing that homelessness itself is traumatic. Staff workflows, supervision, and physical spaces will be designed to reduce burnout and secondary trauma.

### 5. Equity Focused in Service Design and Outcomes

Equity and belonging will be foundational to both service delivery and workplace culture, shaping how clients and staff experience services and connection within programs. Outcomes and experiences will be evaluated through an equity lens. This involves tracking access, engagement, and results across populations to ensure interventions are effective for those most impacted by poverty and systemic exclusion.

### 6. Triage to Appropriate Care

The nonprofit will operate using a hub-and-spoke model to coordinate access to shelter and care for individuals experiencing homelessness in our community. Individuals will first come to the hub for a short-term stay focused on stabilization, assessment, and connection to the shelter setting best aligned with their needs at that time.

The nonprofit will operate or partner with multiple shelter sites, each offering specialized services designed to address common and distinct needs. These sites will include low-barrier shelter, senior shelter with palliative and supportive care, and recovery-oriented shelter.

This model supports cost-effective, specialized care while improving outcomes by ensuring people receive the right level of support without unnecessary delays or transitions. The primary goal of the nonprofit is always to move people into permanent housing as quickly as possible. When needs change, such as during a health crisis or shift in stability, the model allows individuals to transition smoothly between settings to receive care that matches their circumstances over time.

### 7. Access to Housing and Stabilization Pathways

The nonprofit will function as a direct bridge to housing, recognizing that people experiencing street-level homelessness require different levels of intervention to stabilize. For individuals who can quickly regain housing stability, the nonprofit will support access to scattered-site rapid rehousing, delivered in close coordination with shelter and outreach teams to ensure continuity, engagement, and problem-solving support during the transition. For individuals with higher acuity needs who require long-term housing and services, the nonprofit will facilitate access to permanent supportive housing through the region's coordinated entry system. By aligning housing pathways with on-site care, case conferencing, and multidisciplinary review, the nonprofit ensures that housing interventions are timely, appropriate, and responsive to each person's clinical, behavioral health, and stabilization needs.

## Innovation Through Trauma-Informed Design

The nonprofit will apply Trauma-Informed Design principles as a core innovation, treating the built environment as an active part of care rather than a neutral backdrop.

Key design strategies include:

- Nested Layers: offering choice and gradients of privacy, social engagement, and stimulation, supporting people at different stages of crisis, recovery, and stability.
- Identity Anchors: creating spaces that allow personalization, cultural expression, and connection to community and place, countering the institutional feel common in shelters and clinics.

The design goal is to promote healing by creating environments that promote safety, dignity, autonomy, and engagement for both clients and staff.

## Research and Learning Lab

The nonprofit will function as a learning and innovation laboratory, partnering with academic, healthcare, and system stakeholders to study:

- The impact of trauma-informed environments on engagement, crisis reduction, and health outcomes.
- Effective models for integrating shelter, healthcare, and behavioral health.
- Workforce sustainability and trauma-informed operational practices.

Findings will inform programs and contribute to statewide best practices.

## Next Steps

The nonprofit will reduce barriers to care, improve stabilization and engagement, strengthen recovery pathways, and demonstrate how design, care, and systems integration can work together to address street-level homelessness more effectively. To initiate this work, the following is proposed:

- Find an established nonprofit to serve as a fiscal sponsor for 6 months to a year.
- Establish a new nonprofit organization that can respond to regional opportunities to expand shelter services.
- Foster connections with healthcare services including treatment providers and medical services.
- Develop 2 to 3 enhanced shelters within 18 months of startup.
