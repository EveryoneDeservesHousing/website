# EDH Nonprofit Working Brief

This document pulls together the main ideas from the proposal and slide deck into one concise reference.

## Tag-line
A new vision for shelter.
Collective shelter communities.

## Mission

Reimagine how environments, systems, and care models can reduce harm, stabilize people faster, and create clearer pathways to housing, recovery, and long-term health.

## Core Problem Statement

The materials argue that street-level homelessness is not well served by fragmented referrals or standard shelter models alone. The main gaps identified are:

- People living unsheltered need immediate, on-site access to care rather than referral-only pathways.
- Behavioral health and substance use treatment work better when they are co-located and continuous.
- Shelter environments can re-trigger trauma and reduce engagement.
- Staff are exposed to secondary trauma and need safer, more sustainable working environments.

## Proposed Organizational Model

- Build a nonprofit focused specifically on street-level homelessness.
- Integrate shelter, outreach, medical care, behavioral healthcare, recovery pathways, trauma-informed care, trauma-informed design, and applied research into one coordinated model.
- Use the organization as both a service provider and a learning lab.

## Program Elements

### 1. Street-Level Engagement and Shelter Access

- Operate as a front door for people living unsheltered.
- Offer 24/7 access, basic needs, and immediate stabilization without preconditions.
- Coordinate outreach, shelter, and engagement teams as one system.

### 2. Integrated Health and Behavioral Healthcare

- Provide on-site medical, nursing, and behavioral health services.
- Address urgent needs while supporting continuity of care.
- Include mental health treatment, substance use services, medication support, and higher-acuity care partnerships.

### 3. Continuum of Recovery and Treatment

- Avoid a binary treatment model.
- Support medication-assisted treatment, contingency management, recovery groups, and pathways into more intensive treatment when people are ready.

### 4. Trauma-Informed Care and Workforce Support

- Treat homelessness itself as traumatic.
- Design care delivery, supervision, workflows, and physical spaces to reduce burnout and secondary trauma.

### 5. Equity-Focused Services and Workplace Culture

- Make equity and belonging foundational to service delivery and workplace culture.
- Evaluate access, engagement, and outcomes across populations to ensure interventions work for people most impacted by poverty and systemic exclusion.

### 6. Triage to Appropriate Care

- Use a hub-and-spoke model.
- Bring people first to a short-term stabilization hub for assessment and placement.
- Operate or partner with multiple specialized shelter settings.
- Specialized settings named in the materials include low-barrier shelter, senior shelter with palliative and supportive care, and recovery-oriented shelter.
- Allow transitions between settings as needs change.

### 7. Housing and Stabilization Pathways

- Function as a direct bridge to housing.
- For lower-acuity clients, support scattered-site rapid rehousing.
- For higher-acuity clients, facilitate access to permanent supportive housing through coordinated entry.
- Align housing transitions with case conferencing, on-site care, and multidisciplinary review.

## Trauma-Informed Design Strategy

The built environment is treated as part of care rather than neutral infrastructure.

- Nested Layers: give people choice and gradients of privacy, social contact, and stimulation.
- Identity Anchors: create opportunities for personalization, cultural expression, and connection to community and place.

## Research and Learning Agenda

- Study how trauma-informed environments affect engagement, crisis reduction, and health outcomes.
- Study effective models for integrating shelter, healthcare, and behavioral health.
- Study workforce sustainability and trauma-informed operations.
- Use findings to improve programs and contribute to broader best practices.

## Why Create a New Nonprofit

The slide deck gives these reasons:

- Shelter expansion opportunities exist while current provider capacity is limited.
- Experienced leadership may be able to collaborate without competing with existing partners.
- A triage model may improve tailoring of care and reduce costs.

## Organizational Development Tasks

- Secure a fiscal sponsor for roughly 6 to 12 months.
- Incorporate the nonprofit.
- Register as a charity with Washington State.
- Apply for an EIN.
- File for 501(c)(3) status.
- Create bylaws.
- Recruit a board.

## Key Partnership Areas

- Primary care.
- Behavioral health.
- Substance use disorder services, including MOUD, outpatient care, recovery-focused programming, and mobile outreach.
- Culturally responsive services.
- Workforce development for participants.
- Palliative care.
- COPES assessments and home care services.

## Operating Infrastructure Priorities

- Build location-agnostic shelter budgets.
- Define overhead for insurance, HR, accounting, admin, and IT.
- Diversify revenue across public contracts, philanthropy, hospitals, and possible Medicaid/FCS or CoC-related sources.
- Plan for reimbursement gaps and cash flow timing.
- Assign contract compliance ownership.
- Standardize reporting, audit readiness, documentation, intake, eligibility, and dashboards.

## Intended Outcomes

- Reduced barriers to care.
- Faster stabilization and engagement.
- Stronger recovery pathways.
- Better alignment between care setting and client need.
- Quicker movement into permanent housing.
- Lower harm and crisis intensity.
- More sustainable staff experience.

## Near-Term Startup Plan

- Find an established nonprofit to act as fiscal sponsor.
- Establish the new nonprofit so it can pursue regional shelter expansion opportunities.
- Build healthcare and treatment-provider relationships.
- Develop 2 to 3 enhanced shelters within 18 months of startup.
